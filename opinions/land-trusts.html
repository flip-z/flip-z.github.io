Land Trusts

Land trusts claim to preserve open spaces, but in practice, they often feel more like tools for a NIMBY agenda than true conservation efforts. The idea of conserving land has merit, and I don’t question the good intentions of many donors who want to keep natural spaces untouched. But too often, the execution by administrators leans heavily towards keeping new housing developments out rather than truly protecting the land’s ecological or agricultural value.

This approach has real consequences—like driving up housing costs in areas where demand is high. We need new housing to meet demand, not artificial scarcity propped up by land trusts that block development. And while I don’t want subdivisions sprouting up everywhere, there’s a middle ground that land trusts often ignore. Agricultural land should stay in use for farming, not be locked away in a conservation easement that essentially removes it from productive use. A better way forward would be significantly lowering taxes for farmers who keep their land working, making agriculture economically viable without needing to fall back on these restrictive easements.

Conservation easements, at their worst, come off as a wolf in sheep’s clothing—promising to protect land but actually serving to keep neighborhoods exclusive and resistant to growth. Real conservation means balancing land use to include new housing where it’s needed while supporting working lands, not just creating barriers.
